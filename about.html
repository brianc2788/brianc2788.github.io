Programming Experience

Just really quickly, I'd like to talk a little bit about my programming background.

I have been an avid, strong computer user, basically, since I was born. I fondly remember cutting & pasting HTML code from tutorials and into my free website, hosted by Angelfire (Still around!) when I was in 5th grade. Of course, I was an accomplished PC gamer and built myself and my friends custom gaming PCs. We ordered the parts off of a website I found, called Newegg.com (the one-and-only) and we played games like Starcraft and Counter-Strike. I guess I have to mention, we were among the very first Steam users when we transitioned from CS 1.5 to version 1.6.

I had begun to get serious about computer architecture & programming when I decided to enroll at my local community college as a computer science major (CS121). There, I started learning Cpp and Object-Oriented Programming paradigms, but more importantly, I started learning how a computer "thinks" and solves problems. I continued to write in C++, exclusively, for years. I made many console-based apps and eventually scratched the surface of development libraries like SDL2, SFML, and FLTK for GUI's and games.

My second language, Python, was an excellent choice and I was very much enjoying the immediate feedback given by an interpreted language. It's so quick & easy to just run the interpreter (iPython, because I'm a huge CLI-user) and try something out. You can indulge even the most trivial of curiosities with very little effort. I went on to try several more languages like this; including Bash shell scripting and Javascript. I find these languages to be excellent in terms of convenience and productivity; obviously, this is the trade-off for speed & efficiency. Usually sufficing my needs, these languages have largely supplanted my need for C/C++. I've mostly relegated these languages to larger projects (possibly even with GUI front-ends using SDL2) and I dabble in kernel/drivers programming.

I have to give a special mention to shell scripting. After I discovered GNU/Linux, I fell in love with the command-line. I even had access to a MS-DOS based PC in my early years, but I was far too young to use it; I had someone load clip-art or Super Solvers: Midnight Rescue!. I think shell scripting (bash) is an excellent reinforcement for programmers who want to learn Unix-like systems & the command-line interface. In my opinion, from there, it becomes much easier to learn the Windows analogues; the Windows command-prompt, Powershell, the .NET Framework, et al. Linux-based distibutions are the penultimate entry-point to understanding operating systems & kernel design. Ultimately, this would make anyone a better PC user and I would recommend learning shell scripting or Windows batch files to anyone looking to become a power user.

My current muse is golang; the Go programming language. Firstly, I'm a fan of Ken Thompson, who co-authored the language with two other geniuses. Go is a fantastic programming language. In my opinion, it succeeded, in spades, its project's goal, which was to create a modern, efficient, scalable high-level programming language with its own excellent toolchain. That's exactly how I feel about it; it's modern. For me, it's like C/C++ but easier to use due to its built-in mechanisms, packages & modules system, formatting w/tools, and more. Similar to other languages, it has the quality-of-life features that many programmers want, however, still contains the low-level facilities for those who need/want it.

I feel so lucky that, not only have I found something in life that I crave and genuinely love, but I am blessed with the ability to learn it on my own by simply reading documentation, manuals, or other published, non-fiction literature. I find most video tutorials on Youtube or the like to be either only good for beginner fundamentals or not good for anything at all. I'm not kidding; reading comprehension and the ability to follow written instructions is an absolute necessity in the IT industry. Even seasoned veterens will tell you how they are constantly learning & reading to keep up with the ever-evolving world of technology. Beyond being an absolute expert in your programming language(s) of your choice, there is much more that separates the hobbyists from the professionals. What good is a single programmer when he or she can't collaborate with others? Just the word collaboration should invoke names like Git or Slack to mind. Even if one has no intention of working with others, these tools are indispensable whether you are a part of a team or not.

I know I'm not the first to say this, however, I agree 100% that programming languages are tools and each tool has its own strengths and weaknesses. Tools are entirely useless without an effective user. Being capable of helping one's self and others are ingredients for success; I believe that the rest, including the knowledge and skill, will follow. I should also mention that even though I believe in my own words, I still waver from them, make mistakes, and revise it along the way as I grow. Please don't mistake my confidence or conviction with arrogance or hubris; I appreciate wisdom in others and, hopefully, myself when appropriate. At any rate, if this has resonated with you in any way, feel free to get in touch with me.

Thanks for reading and have a wonderful day!
